Tutorial 4: Sinusoidal Wave and the Dirac Delta

Six Sinusoidal Waves and Their Fourier Transforms.

💡
Here we extend the ideas of the second page by considering \(f(x)\) to either be a pure sine wave, a pure cosine wave or a cosine wave with a shift of 45°. Here, "pure" means that the functions are not shifted. Shifting the cosine by 45° produces a sinusoidal wave function which may be decomposed into equivalent cosine and sine components. Additionally, we consider two settings for the signal frequency: \(\beta=\) 0.75 Hz and \(\beta=\) 1.25 Hz. Choose between the six signals and try to reason about the differences of their Fourier transforms.

As we saw on the second page, the Fourier transform of a pure cosine wave only has a real component because the cosine is an even function.
Conversely, the Fourier transform of a pure sine wave only has an imaginary component because the sine is an odd function. The Fourier transform of a sine wave has one positive and one negative peak which may also be explained by the fact that the sine function is odd. This can be seen by comparing the area under the red curve for \(u=-\beta\) and \(u=+\beta\).
The shifted cosine function is the most interesting part of this page. As explained above, this sinusoidal wave consists of equivalent cosine and sine components. Hence, its Fourier transform is composed of a real and an imaginary component.

Regardless of the chosen sinusoidal wave function, the peaks of the Fourier transform always lie at the signal's frequency \(\pm\beta\). The alignment of the frequencies may be observed here as well. To see this, move the frequency slider and let \(u\) slowly approach \(\pm\beta\). While you do this, focus on the blue and red curve on the left.

Note: The examples do not show true Dirac delta functions because we only integrate from -5 to 5 due to visual reasons. If we integrated over infinity, the Fourier transforms would be zero for every frequency except for \(u=\pm\beta\) (where the Dirac delta impulses would lie).
