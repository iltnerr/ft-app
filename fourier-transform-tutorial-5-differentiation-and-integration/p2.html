Tutorial 5: Differentiation and Integration

Differentiation and Integration of Sine and Cosine Signals.

💡
Now we are going to focus on the differentiation and integration of sine and cosine signals. Therefore, we consider the two functions \(\sin(2\pi\beta x)\) and \(\cos(2\pi\beta x)\). You may choose the function and the mode (Normal, Differentiation or Integration) with the provided buttons.

Normal
  • \(f(x) = \sin(2\pi\beta x)\)
  • \(f(x) = \cos(2\pi\beta x)\)
The Fourier transform of a pure sine wave and a pure cosine wave consists of either two imaginary or two real Dirac delta impulses (see Tutorial 4).

Integration
  • \(f(x) = \int \sin(2\pi\beta x)\,\mathrm{d}x = -\cos(2\pi\beta x)\cdot \frac{1}{2\pi\beta}\)
  • \(f(x) = \int \cos(2\pi\beta x)\,\mathrm{d}x = \sin(2\pi\beta x)\cdot \frac{1}{2\pi\beta}\)
Consider the following aspects:
  1. Due to the integration, the amplitude of \(f(x)\) decreases with growing frequency.
  2. According to the integration property of the Fourier transform, \(F(u)\) has to be divided by \(j\omega\).
Therefore, as the amplitude of \(f(x)\) decreases with the frequency, so do the Dirac delta impulses.
Note: To avoid dividing by zero, the frequency slider is limited to 1 rad/s = \(\frac{1}{2\pi}\) Hz.

Differentiation
  • \(f(x) = \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\,\big(\sin(2\pi\beta x)\big) = \cos(2\pi\beta x)\cdot 2\pi\beta \)
  • \(f(x) = \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\,\big(\cos(2\pi\beta x)\big) = -\sin(2\pi\beta x)\cdot 2\pi\beta \)
Consider the following aspects:
  1. Due to the differentiation, the amplitude of \(f(x)\) increases with growing frequency.
  2. According to the differentiation property of the Fourier transform, \(F(u)\) has to be multiplied by \(j\omega\).
Therefore, as the amplitude of \(f(x)\) increases with the frequency, so do the Dirac delta impulses.
Note: Since the responses of \(f(x)\) and \(F(u)\) increase with growing frequency, the y-axes have to be rescaled accordingly in case of differentiation.

Summary

 Through integration or differentiation, even functions become odd and odd functions become even.
The same is true for the real and imaginary components of \(F(u)\). By integrating or differentiating \(f(x)\), a real-valued Fourier transform becomes imaginary-valued and vice versa.
